What Are We Learning?

🏢

Structures are all around us! A structure is something that has been built to hold or support something. Think about your school building, bridges, houses, and playground equipment.

🌍

Every structure we build has an effect on our society and our environment. Society means the people and communities we live in. The environment is the natural world around us.

👷

Structures help people in many ways. They give us places to live, work, and play. They help us travel. They keep us safe from weather. These are positive effects on society!

🌳

Building can have negative effects on the environment. We might cut down trees, use up natural resources like wood and metal, and create pollution. Construction machines create smoke.

♻️

People are finding better ways to build! We can use recycled materials, build green buildings that use less energy, protect animal habitats, and plant new trees.

Examples

🏫

Schools

Give students a place to learn. Building uses lots of materials. Solution: Use solar panels and natural light!

🌉

Bridges

Help people cross rivers safely. Can block fish from swimming upstream. Solution: Build fish ladders!

🏡

Houses

Provide shelter for families. Can be built on animal habitats. Solution: Use eco-friendly materials!

🎢

Playgrounds

Give children places to play. Old equipment can have harmful chemicals. Solution: Use recycled plastic!

What Are We Learning?

🐝

Did you know that animals build structures too? Bees build hives, birds build nests, beavers build dams, and spiders build webs. These animal structures are just as important!

🦫

Animal structures have different effects than human structures. When a beaver builds a dam, it creates a pond. This new pond can help some plants and animals.

🏗️

Human structures often have a bigger impact on the environment. We use machines, cut down many trees at once, and build very large structures. We use materials like concrete and steel.

🌱

Animal structures usually work with nature. A bird's nest uses twigs and grass that will break down naturally. A beaver dam uses branches and mud.

💡

We can learn from how animals build! Scientists study animal structures to find better ways for humans to build. This is called biomimicry.

Examples

🦫

Beavers

Build dams and lodges from sticks and mud. Create wetlands that help many plants and animals. Can flood areas and change water flow.

🐝

Honeybees

Build hives with hexagon-shaped cells from beeswax. Help pollinate plants. Use very little space and resources.

🐜

Ants

Build underground tunnels and hills. Help mix and aerate soil which is good for plants. Don't harm the environment around them.

🐦

Birds

Build nests from twigs, grass, and feathers. Use natural materials that break down easily. Build in trees without cutting them down.

What Are We Learning?

🏗️

A structure is a supporting framework that holds a load and has a definite size, shape, and function. This means a structure supports weight, has a specific size and shape, and does a job!

🌲

Structures can be found in the natural environment. Trees are natural structures that hold up branches and leaves. Mountains are structures. Your skeleton is a natural structure inside your body!

🏢

Structures can be in the built environment. The built environment means things that people have made. Houses, schools, bridges, chairs, and desks are all built structures.

📏

Every structure has three important things: size, shape, and function. The size tells us how big it is. The shape tells us what it looks like. The function tells us what job it does.

⚖️

All structures hold a load. A load is the weight that a structure has to support. Your chair holds your body. A bridge holds cars and trucks. A tree holds its own branches and leaves!

Examples

🏫

School Building

Size: Large, multi-room. Shape: Rectangular with rooms. Function: Provide space for learning. Load: Students, teachers, desks, books.

🪑

Chair

Size: Small, fits one person. Shape: Seat with legs and back. Function: Provide a place to sit. Load: Person sitting on it.

🌉

Bridge

Size: Long and wide. Shape: Flat surface supported by beams. Function: Help people cross water. Load: Cars, trucks, people.

📚

Bookshelf

Size: Tall with levels. Shape: Vertical with horizontal shelves. Function: Store and organize books. Load: Many books and items.

What Are We Learning?

🎯

Form and function means that the shape of a structure (its form) matches what it needs to do (its function). The way something looks should help it do its job better!

🚗

Think about a car. Its form is a streamlined shape low to the ground. Its function is to move people quickly and safely. The smooth form helps it cut through air and go faster!

🏠

A house has a triangular roof. Why? The triangular form allows rain and snow to slide off easily. If roofs were flat, water could pile up. The triangle shape helps the roof do its job!

🪑

Different structures have different forms because they have different functions. A stool has no back for quick sitting. An office chair has wheels and armrests for comfortable long sitting.

🦒

Even in nature, form follows function! A giraffe has a very long neck (form) so it can reach leaves high up in trees (function). Nature is an expert at matching form and function!

Examples

✏️

Pencil

Form: Long, thin cylinder with pointed end. Function: Write and draw. The pointed tip creates clear marks, and the long shape is easy to hold!

☂️

Umbrella

Form: Dome or curved canopy. Function: Keep rain off you. The curved dome makes water run off the sides instead of pooling on top!

🌉

Arch Bridge

Form: Curved arch shape. Function: Support weight while spanning a gap. The arch spreads weight evenly down to the supports!

🪜

Ladder

Form: Two vertical poles with horizontal steps. Function: Help people climb up high. Steps provide footholds, and vertical poles keep it stable!

What Are We Learning?

💪

The strength of a structure is its ability to support a load without breaking or bending too much. A strong structure can hold a lot of weight!

🧱

We can make structures stronger by using stronger materials. Concrete and steel are very strong materials that can hold heavy loads. Wood is also strong, especially when thick.

📐

The shape of a structure affects its strength. Triangles are one of the strongest shapes because they don't change shape easily when force is applied. That's why you see triangles in bridges!

📏

Making materials thicker can increase strength. A thick wooden beam is stronger than a thin one. More material means more strength to resist bending and breaking!

🏗️

Adding supports and braces can make structures stronger. A table gets wobbly with only corner legs. Adding diagonal braces makes the table much stronger and prevents wobbling!

Examples

🧱

Use Strong Materials

Steel, concrete, and thick wood can support heavy loads. Steel is stronger than plastic. Concrete is stronger than cardboard. Choosing the right material matters!

📏

Make It Thicker

Thicker materials are stronger. A thick book is harder to bend than a single page. A thick rope holds more weight. Thickness adds strength!

🔺

Use Triangles

Triangular shapes are very strong and don't collapse easily. Bridges use triangular trusses. Towers have triangular supports. Triangles spread forces evenly!

🔩

Add Supports

Extra beams, braces, and columns add strength. A shelf with a center support holds more books. A chair with cross-braces is sturdier. More supports mean more strength!

What Are We Learning?

⚖️

The stability of a structure is its ability to keep its shape, maintain balance, float, or stay fixed when a force is applied. A stable structure doesn't tip over or wobble easily!

🎯

The center of gravity is important for stability. This is the point where all weight is balanced. If the center of gravity is low and centered, the structure is more stable!

📐

A wide base makes a structure more stable. Think about a pyramid - it has a very wide base and gets narrower at top. This makes it hard to knock over!

⚓

Structures can be more stable by being fixed or anchored to something. A tent uses stakes in the ground. A bookshelf attached to the wall won't tip over!

🛟

Some structures need to float to be stable. Boats are designed with hulls that displace water and keep them floating upright. The shape and weight distribution matter!

Examples

📐

Widen the Base

A wider base provides more support and makes it harder to tip. Imagine pushing over a cone - it's easier upside down (narrow base) than right side up!

⬇️

Lower Center of Gravity

Put heavy things on bottom and light things on top. A truck carrying rocks on bottom is more stable than with rocks piled high on top!

🔗

Anchor or Attach

Fixing structures prevents tipping. Tents use stakes, tall buildings have deep foundations, and bookshelves can be secured to walls!

⚖️

Balance the Weight

Distribute weight evenly on all sides. A backpack with heavy books on one side makes you lean. Spreading weight evenly keeps you balanced!

What Are We Learning?

🧱

When building structures, we need to think about the properties of materials. Properties are the special characteristics that materials have, like being hard, soft, flexible, or rigid.

💪

Strength is an important property. Strong materials like steel and concrete can hold heavy loads. Weak materials like paper and thin plastic cannot hold much weight.

🔄

Flexibility means a material can bend without breaking. Rubber and fabric are flexible. Rigidity means a material doesn't bend easily. Wood and metal are rigid materials.

💧

Some materials are waterproof while others absorb water. Plastic and metal don't absorb water, making them good for outdoor structures. Wood can absorb water and might rot.

🌡️

Materials react differently to temperature. Some materials expand when hot and contract when cold. Builders must consider how materials will behave in different weather!

Examples

🧱

Concrete

Properties: Very strong, rigid, heavy, waterproof. Good for: Foundations, walls, roads. Not good for: Flexible items, lightweight structures.

🪵

Wood

Properties: Strong, can be flexible, lighter than concrete, absorbs water. Good for: Frames, furniture, floors. Not good for: Underwater structures.

🔩

Steel

Properties: Very strong, rigid, heavy, waterproof, conducts heat. Good for: Bridges, beams, tall buildings. Not good for: Lightweight needs.

🧵

Fabric

Properties: Flexible, lightweight, can be waterproof, soft. Good for: Tents, awnings, sails. Not good for: Supporting heavy loads.

What Are We Learning?

💨

Forces are pushes and pulls that can affect structures. Different forces can change the shape, balance, or position of a structure. Understanding forces helps us build better!

⬇️

Gravity is a force that pulls everything down toward the ground. Gravity makes structures heavy and pulls them down. Builders must design structures to resist gravity's pull!

💨

Wind is a force that pushes against structures. Strong winds can push buildings, blow over fences, and even damage weak structures. Wind is especially strong on tall buildings!

🌊

Water can apply force to structures. Flowing water pushes against dams and bridges. Waves push against docks and piers. Structures near water must be built to resist these forces!

🏋️

Compression and tension are important forces. Compression squeezes and pushes materials together. Tension pulls and stretches materials apart. Different parts of structures experience different forces!

Examples

⬇️

Gravity

Effect: Pulls structures downward. Example: A bridge must be strong enough to resist gravity pulling it down while holding cars. Solution: Strong materials and supports.

💨

Wind

Effect: Pushes against structures sideways. Example: Tall buildings sway in strong winds. Solution: Deep foundations and flexible materials that can move slightly.

❄️

Snow and Ice

Effect: Add weight and pressure. Example: Heavy snow on roofs can make them collapse. Solution: Sloped roofs and strong beams to hold weight.

🌊

Water Pressure

Effect: Pushes against structures. Example: Dams must withstand tons of water pushing against them. Solution: Very thick, strong concrete walls.

What Are We Learning?

🔩

Struts and ties are important parts of structures under load. They work together to make structures strong and stable. Understanding them helps us build better structures!

📏

A strut is a part that pushes or supports by resisting compression. Struts are usually straight and rigid. They hold things up by being pushed on. Think of the legs of a table!

🪢

A tie is a part that pulls or supports by resisting tension. Ties are usually flexible and can be pulled. They hold things together by being pulled on. Think of ropes holding up a tent!

🌉

In bridges, struts and ties work together. The beams that hold the bridge up are struts (they're compressed). The cables that pull parts together are ties (they're in tension).

🏗️

Struts and ties are everywhere! Chair legs are struts. Suspension bridge cables are ties. Your skeleton has bones (struts) and muscles and tendons (ties) working together!

Examples

🪑

Chair Legs (Struts)

Role: Support your weight by resisting compression. Effect: When you sit, the legs are pushed on and compressed. They must be rigid to not collapse.

🎪

Tent Ropes (Ties)

Role: Hold tent in place by resisting tension. Effect: The ropes are pulled tight and must not break. They can be flexible like rope.

🌉

Bridge Beams (Struts)

Role: Support the bridge deck by resisting compression. Effect: The weight of cars pushes down on beams. They must be strong and rigid.

🪁

Kite String (Tie)

Role: Hold kite by resisting tension. Effect: Wind pulls on the kite and stretches the string. String must be strong enough not to break.
